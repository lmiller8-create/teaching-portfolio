Teaching Metaphor

"Teaching is like being a coach on game day, every single day."

A coach doesn't play the game for the athletes. They prepare them, challenge them, believe in them when they don't quite believe in themselves yet, and then step back and let them show what they've got. That's what I want to do in a classroom.

The Metaphor Explained

I've been a soccer player my whole life, and a team captain for the past two years. What I've learned from that is that the best coaches I've had weren't the loudest or the most demanding. They were the ones who figured out what each individual needed and gave it to them. One player needs to be pushed hard. Another needs someone to quietly build their confidence. A third just needs you to get out of their way and trust them.

That's exactly how I think about teaching. Every kid in a classroom is different. They come in with different skills, different fears, different reasons for being there. A teacher who treats everyone the same isn't being fair. They're being lazy. Real fairness means figuring out what each student actually needs to succeed and giving them that.

And just like a coach, a teacher has to be honest. You can't just tell kids they're doing great when they're not. Real growth comes from honest feedback, given with care. That's a balance I'm still learning, but it's one I take seriously.

Teaching Philosophy

What I Believe About Learning

I believe a classroom should be a place where students feel comfortable and actually want to be there. Learning isn't just about getting good grades. It's about building confidence and figuring out how to think for yourself. I don't just want to stand at the front and talk. I want to create an environment where students feel supported and aren't afraid to speak up or make mistakes.

I think students learn best when they're actually interested in what they're doing. If everything is just notes and lectures, people are going to check out. So I'd keep things more interactive with group work, discussions, and hands on activities. When students can connect what they're learning to real life it sticks better and they care more about it.

Planning and Assessment

When it comes to planning, I'd use standards so I know what I'm aiming for, but I'm not going to teach everything the exact same way to every student. Everyone learns differently and at different speeds, so I'd switch things up depending on what the class needs. Some students need more help, some are ready to move ahead, and that's normal.

For assessment I don't think it should just be tests all the time. I'd use smaller check ins, classwork, and projects to see how students are doing along the way. That way I can catch problems early instead of waiting until the end when it's too late. I care more about students improving than just getting a score.

Technology is something I'd use but not overdo. Videos or online tools can make things more interesting and help students who struggle with traditional ways of learning. But it shouldn't be a distraction or replace actually teaching.

Inclusion and Every Student

Making sure every student feels included is something I take seriously. Not everyone comes from the same background and not everyone learns the same way. That's why things like accommodations and IEPs matter. If a student needs extra time, different assignments, or support in class then that should be provided, no question. It's not about making things easier. It's about giving students a fair chance to succeed.

I also think teachers need to work with others and not try to do everything alone. Things like IEP teams, RTI, and working with parents are all part of helping students succeed. If a student is struggling it's important to track progress, communicate with others, and actually do something about it instead of ignoring it.

Where I'm at Right Now

At the end of the day I just want to be the kind of teacher that students respect and feel comfortable around. Someone who actually cares and pays attention to what students need. I know I'm still learning and my philosophy will probably change over time, but right now my goal is simple: create a classroom where students feel supported, included, and motivated to improve.

Classroom Observations

These are reflections from my Atlas classroom observations completed in my teacher preparation coursework. Each one asked me to connect what I was watching to the concepts we were reading about in class.

Observation #1

Chapter 1: Becoming a Teacher: Looking Forward and Backward at the Same Time

Video Selected
Experimenting with Force and Motion through Roller Coaster Designs, a fifth-grade lesson where students built and tested roller coasters as a way to explore physics and math together.
How Was the Classroom Set Up?
The desks were arranged in small groups, not rows. Each group had assigned roles like timer, recorder, and manager so everyone had a job. It felt less like a classroom and more like a workspace. That kind of setup matched what our chapter talks about when it describes student-centered learning: when kids have real roles and real responsibilities, they actually stay engaged.
What Was the Teacher Doing?
She wasn't at the front of the room lecturing. She was moving between groups asking things like "Why didn't that trial work?" or "What would you change?" She let students struggle and figure stuff out before stepping in. That's exactly what the chapter means when it talks about guiding learning through questions instead of just handing out answers. She trusted the process, which is harder than it sounds.
What Were the Students Doing?
They were actually problem-solving, not pretending to. When a roller coaster design flopped, they'd talk through what went wrong (friction, not enough momentum, whatever it was) and then try again. They were also doing real math, collecting data and calculating speed. The chapter talks a lot about active engagement being the key to real learning, and this was a good example of what that actually looks like versus what it sounds like on paper.
The Five Qualities of a Good Teacher (Chapter 1, pg. 10)
Commitment to student learning: The teacher built the unit starting with simpler activities and working up to the full roller coaster design. That was clearly intentional scaffolding.

Caring toward students: When groups hit a wall, she didn't criticize them. She encouraged them to keep going and pointed out when they were working well together. She said something like "I can see you're using good teamwork" and that kind of small comment matters more than people think.

Conscious about what's going on: She noticed which students were quieter or still learning English and made a point to ask them questions directly. She wasn't just paying attention to the kids who were loudest. She was watching everyone.

Centered and calm: Nothing seemed to rattle her. When activities failed multiple times, she stayed steady. That kept the students from getting frustrated and giving up, which honestly might be the hardest thing to do consistently as a teacher.

Courageous about sticking with the plan: She didn't jump in to save struggling groups. She let them sit in the discomfort of not knowing and figure it out, which takes real confidence in the process.
Classroom Culture and Climate
The whole room had this vibe where it was okay to fail and try again. Kids were admitting things weren't working and nobody made it weird. They just kept going. That's not something that happens by accident. The chapter talks about how a positive classroom climate builds student confidence, and you could see that playing out. These kids weren't afraid to be wrong, which is honestly rare.
A Question This Raised for Me
How do you make sure every student stays engaged during group work without micromanaging them? There's a real tension between letting kids struggle productively and noticing when someone has checked out completely.
What I Took Away from This Observation
Letting students struggle productively actually leads to better learning and a stronger classroom culture. That sounds obvious, but watching it happen made it real. The teacher could have jumped in to fix things a dozen times and she didn't, and the students were better for it.
Observation #2

Chapter 2: Teaching Stories: Individual Paths into Education

Video Selected
Using Images to Build Speaking, Listening, and Descriptive Language Skills
A Challenging Moment in the Lesson
One of the hardest things the teacher had to manage was getting students to use real academic vocabulary instead of just defaulting to basic descriptions like color or size rather than specific scientific terminology. A lot of students naturally described whales that way, which made sense, but the lesson was asking for more than that. The teacher had to intentionally build background knowledge and model the correct language structures before students could get there. On top of that, managing a room with different language levels while keeping everyone engaged made the challenge even bigger.
An Exciting or Rewarding Moment
The gallery walk stood out to me as the most energizing part of the lesson. Students were actually moving around the room, looking at whale images, and recording their observations with partners. The digital voice recorders added something and they made the activity feel real and engaging rather than just another worksheet. What seemed most rewarding was watching students restate their partner's ideas clearly, and even catch and self-correct their own grammar while they were talking. You could see the confidence building in real time.
Hidden Curriculum (Chapter Reading Connection, pg. 32)
Yes, and it was actually one of the most interesting things to notice once I was looking for it. The students weren't just learning about whales. They were learning how to listen without interrupting, how to support a partner who communicates differently than them, how to speak in front of other people without falling apart. Those skills weren't listed anywhere as learning objectives, but they were being taught and reinforced the whole time. That's the hidden curriculum, the social and emotional lessons that run underneath the academic ones.
A Question This Raised for Me
How can a teacher keep challenging advanced students while also giving enough structure and support to English learners who need more scaffolding? I kept watching the teacher try to do both at once and wondering how you actually manage that without one group getting left behind.
What I Took Away from This Observation
Building background knowledge and modeling language expectations before asking students to produce language makes a real difference. When students know what's expected of them, have the vocabulary they need, and have had a chance to practice in a low-stakes way, they show up to the harder task with actual confidence. That seems obvious when you say it out loud, but watching it play out in a real classroom made it click in a way that just reading about it didn't.

Rubrics and Standards

These are the frameworks and standards that guide my development as a pre-service teacher through my credential program at CSU Chico.

CORE Academic Skills Assessment

California Basic Skills Requirement

The California Subject Examinations for Teachers (CSET) and the CORE Multiple Subjects exam are required assessments for teacher candidates in California. They assess foundational academic skills in reading, writing, and mathematics that teachers need to support student learning across content areas.

The CORE exam is designed to ensure that teacher candidates have the academic knowledge and skills needed to help all students, including English learners and students with special needs, to develop foundational literacy and numeracy skills.

ReadingFoundational literacy, comprehension, and analysis skills
WritingComposition, mechanics, and written communication
MathematicsNumber concepts, algebra, and mathematical reasoning

Teacher Performance Expectations (TPEs)

California Commission on Teacher Credentialing

The California TPEs define what beginning teachers are expected to know and be able to do. They're organized into six domains and are the foundation of how student teachers are evaluated during fieldwork and clinical practice. My work in this portfolio connects to several of these domains directly.

Disposition Rubric

CSU Chico School of Education

The Disposition Rubric evaluates the professional attitudes, values, and behaviors that teacher candidates demonstrate throughout their preparation program. Dispositions aren't just about what you know. They're about who you are in a classroom and how you show up for students, families, and colleagues.

The areas assessed include professionalism, commitment to equity, collaboration, reflective practice, and communication. I take these seriously not just as things to be graded on, but as the actual qualities that define what it means to be a good teacher.
